# Accelerators × Foundations Integration Map

## How the Two Frameworks Relate

| Framework | Role |
|---|---|
| **Foundations** | Theological and missiological *guardrails* — the why, who, and non-negotiable how |
| **Phases of Engagement** | *Progress measurement* — where a people group is on the journey |
| **Accelerators** | *Catalytic levers* — what can move a group toward the next phase |

Accelerators are valid tools **only insofar as they serve the Missionary Task within Foundations' constraints.** This map makes that relationship explicit for each of the 12 domains.

**Integration principle:** When an Accelerator application would compromise biblical faithfulness, create dependency, bypass local church accountability, or soften the gospel, the Foundations guardrail supersedes the Accelerator suggestion.

---

## Mapping Key

- **Missionary Task component(s):** Which of the 6 Foundations components this Accelerator primarily serves
- **Foundations alignment:** Clean (maps directly) / Reframe (same idea, different language) / Guardrail (tension requiring constraint)
- **Applied rule:** How to use this Accelerator within Foundations' framework

---

## The 12 Accelerators

---

### 1. Prayer
**Missionary Task:** Permeates all 6 components (not a component itself)

**Foundations alignment: Reframe**

Foundations elevates prayer *above* the other accelerators — it is not one of 12 equal tools but the atmosphere of the entire task: "Prayer must permeate every aspect of the missionary task." The Accelerator treats prayer as one of 12 diagnostic domains; Foundations treats it as the oxygen.

**Applied rule:** Prayer is not a gap to diagnose and close — it is an assumed posture. In practice, lead every phase assessment and accelerator discernment process with prayer, not as a step to check off but as the mode of the whole exercise. The Accelerator question ("who could join you in praying?") is good; just don't frame it as one of 12 equal levers.

---

### 2. Scripture/Resource Access
**Missionary Task:** Entry (communication ability, language), Evangelism (full gospel content), Discipleship (obedience to Scripture)

**Foundations alignment: Clean**

Foundations is deeply aligned: heart-language Scripture access is essential at every phase. Foundations adds that Bible translation and gospel content must maintain full theological integrity — no de-emphasizing of the deity of Christ, the cross, or salvation by grace through faith alone.

**Applied rule:** Scripture access efforts must prioritize theological accuracy over speed or cultural palatability. Foundations explicitly says "we cannot leave out or change elements of the gospel message simply because they offend non-Christian hearers." This applies to translated and localized resources, not just live proclamation.

---

### 3. Multi-Node Engagement
**Missionary Task:** Evangelism, Entry (presence across geography/demographics)

**Foundations alignment: Clean**

Consistent with Foundations' priority on reaching unreached peoples and places. Engaging multiple nodes broadens gospel presence and reduces fragility — this aligns with Foundations' emphasis on teams and collaborative reach rather than lone-worker dependency.

**Applied rule:** Node selection should be driven by access to the focus people group, not primarily by ease or existing relationships with expats or diaspora. Foundations' Entry guidance applies: maximize actual access to the people being reached.

---

### 4. Vision Casting
**Missionary Task:** Mobilization/Sending (upstream), Collaborative Engagement

**Foundations alignment: Reframe**

The Accelerator frames vision casting around "stories and simple data" to align teams and sustain momentum — this is strategically sound. Foundations, however, roots vision in the glory of God and the weight of lostness (eternal condemnation, no other means of salvation), not primarily in data or momentum narrative.

**Applied rule:** Vision casting in this framework should lead with theological gravity — the glory of God, the reality of lostness, the urgency of proclamation — before moving to strategic clarity. Data and story serve that vision; they don't constitute it. Strip any framing that reduces vision to organizational alignment or engagement metrics.

---

### 5. Mobilization/Sending
**Missionary Task:** Entry (presence), team structure throughout all 6 components

**Foundations alignment: Clean with guardrail**

Strong alignment: Foundations is explicitly committed to team-based sending, member care, and the critical role of mobilizers. The guardrail: Foundations requires missionaries to be sent *by and accountable to local churches*, not merely deployed through organizational pipelines. Sending is ecclesiological, not just logistical.

**Applied rule:** Mobilization pipelines must be church-rooted. Workers should be examined, affirmed, and sent by a local church, with ongoing accountability to that church. Mobilization that bypasses local church accountability — even if practically efficient — is outside Foundations' framework. This applies especially to near-culture and local workers being sent out in Phases 5–6.

---

### 6. Collaborative Engagement
**Missionary Task:** Runs across all 6 components; Foundations emphasizes working alongside other evangelical churches and agencies

**Foundations alignment: Clean with guardrail**

Foundations actively encourages working with other evangelical churches and organizations. The guardrail: Foundations is specific that collaboration must not compromise theological, ecclesiological, or missiological alignment. It distinguishes between:
- Work *within and through* indigenous churches that are healthy in all three areas
- Work *alongside* churches healthy theologically/ecclesiologically but not missiologically
- Work *around* churches that are unhealthy — maintaining relationship but not structural partnership

**Applied rule:** Collaborative agreements should clarify alignment on theology, ecclesiology, and missiology before deepening partnership. Shared prayer, data, and learning are low-barrier entry points. Structural collaboration (co-planting, shared teams) requires greater alignment. Don't collapse collaboration into networking — Foundations is precise about what partnership means.

---

### 7. Meeting Needs/Compassion
**Missionary Task:** Not one of the 6 components; tangentially present in Entry (servant posture, host community blessing)

**Foundations alignment: Guardrail — highest tension**

This is the most significant point of friction. Foundations' 6-component Missionary Task does not include compassion ministry as a component. Foundations frames the task as fundamentally proclamation-centered: people must hear, understand, and believe the gospel to be saved. Compassion has a place — serving the host community, being a blessing — but it is subordinate to and in service of gospel proclamation, never a replacement or precursor that stands alone.

The Accelerator's language ("tangible expressions of love that dignify communities and open relational doors") is acceptable *if* the relational doors lead to gospel engagement. The Accelerator's own guardrail ("do no harm, locally led, connect naturally to long-term discipleship") helps.

**Applied rule:** Compassion activities are valid when they:
1. Serve the people group being reached (not primarily expat or donor communities)
2. Are locally led and avoid creating dependency on foreign resources or expertise
3. Connect intentionally to ongoing gospel engagement and discipleship
4. Do not substitute for proclamation or become the primary identity of the team

Compassion that creates dependency, distracts from the missionary task, or positions the worker primarily as a development worker rather than a gospel worker falls outside Foundations' framework. Flag this clearly in training and use-case content.

---

### 8. Critical Contextualization
**Missionary Task:** All 6 components; Foundations' Missiological Conviction on Biblical Contextualization and Indigenization

**Foundations alignment: Guardrail — significant**

The Accelerator's definition — "community-led application of Scripture that is biblically faithful and culturally meaningful, reviewing forms and practices with diverse voices" — is broadly consistent with Foundations. But the language "community-led" and "guard against drift and syncretism" signals the risk that Foundations addresses directly and at length.

Foundations' hard limits on contextualization:
- **Form can be adapted; content cannot.** Gospel message, doctrinal content, and Christian identity are non-negotiable.
- **Never de-emphasize:** the One True God (Father, Son, Holy Spirit), the deity of Christ, His death and resurrection, substitutionary atonement, salvation by grace through faith alone, Scripture's integrity and finality, or the radical nature of conversion.
- **Clear Christian identity required:** Churches must not present themselves as essentially part of any non-Christian religion. Workers must not deny their identity as followers of Jesus.
- **Goal is acceptable outsiders, not invisible insiders.** Foundations explicitly cautions against adopting local religious identity to the point of obscuring the gospel.

**Applied rule:** Contextualization work must be evaluated by the question: *Does this make the gospel clearer, or does it make it more comfortable?* The purpose is clarity, not palatability. Any contextualization proposal that softens hard edges of the gospel, blurs Christian identity, or implies continuity with non-Christian religion is outside Foundations' framework. This guardrail should be explicitly taught in training content.

---

### 9. Research/Cultural Insights
**Missionary Task:** Entry (research is the first element — who, where, what history, what language/culture, what existing work)

**Foundations alignment: Clean**

Direct alignment. Foundations treats research as foundational to Entry and calls for structures and processes to coordinate it. Research informs language learning, cultural engagement, strategy, and partnership decisions across all phases.

**Applied rule:** Research is not a one-time entry activity — it's an ongoing learning posture (as the Accelerator frames it). The Foundations emphasis: research serves the missionary task, not organizational reporting. Use research to sharpen evangelism, discipleship, and church planting strategy, not just to populate databases.

---

### 10. Multiplying Efforts
**Missionary Task:** Healthy Church Formation, Leadership Development, Exit to Partnership

**Foundations alignment: Clean with guardrail**

Strong alignment with Foundations' conviction that "the most effective way to advance the gospel into the unreached world is to multiply churches that multiply churches." Exit to Partnership is the destination — the missionary's goal is to work themselves out of a role by raising up indigenous leaders and self-sustaining church networks.

The guardrail: multiplication must be of *healthy* churches (12 Characteristics), not just any reproducing group. Speed of reproduction without health produces fragile or syncretistic networks.

**Applied rule:** Multiplying Efforts must be evaluated against church health, not just reproduction metrics. A multiplying network that lacks biblical teaching, accountability, or the ordinances is not a success state — it's a Phase 6 that requires discipleship investment. Wise release of authority (as the Accelerator notes) means raising up indigenous leaders who can maintain health, not just hand off and exit.

---

### 11. Training/Equipping
**Missionary Task:** Leadership Development (primary), Discipleship

**Foundations alignment: Clean**

Direct alignment. Foundations' Leadership Development component is about raising up local leaders who can govern, finance, propagate, and theologize indigenously. Training/equipping that is reproducible, practitioner-delivered, and designed to be passed on maps well.

**Applied rule:** Training content must target indigeneity as the outcome — not dependence on outside trainers or foreign curriculum. Foundations' standard: churches should be self-theologizing, meaning trained leaders can apply Scripture to their own context without ongoing foreign expert input. Training programs that create long-term trainer dependency fall short of this goal.

---

### 12. Marketplace Involvement
**Missionary Task:** Entry (creative access, presence, identity)

**Foundations alignment: Clean with guardrail**

Foundations explicitly addresses Business as Mission (BAM) and creative access contexts. Its requirements:
- **Integrity:** Actually do what you say you do. The access role must be performed with genuine credibility.
- **Fit:** Workers should choose access strategies consistent with their actual skills and interests.
- **Access to people:** The role should maximize contact with the focus people group, not primarily with expats or screens.
- **Identity:** Workers must have a ready, honest answer to "what do you do?" and must never deny their identity as followers of Jesus.

**Applied rule:** Marketplace involvement is a valid Entry and presence strategy, not a long-term cover. It must provide genuine relational access to the people group, be performed with integrity, and not become so consuming that it crowds out the missionary task itself. Any marketplace model that requires denying or hiding Christian identity falls outside Foundations' framework.

---

## Summary: Alignment Tiers

| Tier | Accelerators | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Clean alignment** | Research/Cultural Insights, Scripture/Resource Access, Multi-Node Engagement, Training/Equipping | Direct mapping to Missionary Task components; no material tension |
| **Reframe needed** | Prayer, Vision Casting | Valid tools but require reframing within Foundations' theological weight |
| **Guardrail required** | Mobilization/Sending, Collaborative Engagement, Multiplying Efforts, Marketplace Involvement | Good alignment but specific Foundations constraints must be applied |
| **Significant guardrail** | Critical Contextualization, Meeting Needs/Compassion | Most friction with Foundations; require explicit teaching and careful framing in training content |

## The Phase 4 Threshold: The Indigeneity Shift

Phase 4 is not just a milestone — it is a **transfer of primary agency.** Before Phase 4, outside workers (missionaries, near-culture believers, mobilizers) are the primary actors. At Phase 4 and beyond, local believers and local churches become the primary agents, and Foundations has extensive, non-negotiable guidance on what this must look like.

### Foundations' Four Selfs (the indigeneity standard)

Foundations states that new churches should be:
- **Self-governing** — Led by local elders/leaders, not under the authority of the missionary or foreign organization
- **Self-financing** — Not dependent on foreign funds for ongoing operation
- **Self-propagating** — Reproducing through local initiative and local sending
- **Self-theologizing** — Able to apply Scripture to their own context without ongoing dependence on foreign experts

These are not aspirational ideals — Foundations calls them the "classical missiological principles of indigeneity" and treats dependency on foreign leadership and foreign funds as "deadly to the health, growth, and multiplication of churches."

### How This Maps to Phases 4–7

| Phase | Indigeneity Marker | Missionary Task Shift |
|---|---|---|
| **4 – Local Church** | Local believers gathering; local leaders *emerging* | Missionary moves from planter to nurturer; role is raising up, not leading |
| **5 – Reproducing Church** | Local churches *sending* their own planters | Missionary role recedes further; coaching and accountability from outside |
| **6 – Multiplying Church** | Local network structures *empowering local oversight* | Missionary is at most a distant partner; Exit to Partnership is in view |
| **7 – Sustained Gospel Presence** | Local networks *sending to other peoples* | Full Exit to Partnership; local church is now a sending church |

### How Each Accelerator Shifts at Phase 4+

The Accelerators don't disappear after Phase 4 — they transfer to new hands. The question changes from *"what should workers do?"* to *"what should local churches and leaders do, and how does outside support serve (not substitute for) that?"*

| Accelerator | Phases 0–3 (outside-led) | Phases 4–7 (locally-led) |
|---|---|---|
| **Prayer** | Workers interceding for the people group | Local believers interceding for their own people and outward |
| **Scripture/Resource Access** | Workers securing translation, distribution | Local church owning, evaluating, and distributing resources in their language |
| **Multi-Node Engagement** | Workers identifying and entering new nodes | Local churches sending workers to new nodes from within |
| **Vision Casting** | Workers and mobilizers casting vision for the people group | Local leaders holding and articulating vision for their own people |
| **Mobilization/Sending** | Outside agencies recruiting and sending workers in | Local churches identifying, preparing, and sending their own workers out |
| **Collaborative Engagement** | Outside agencies coordinating with one another | Local churches as primary collaborators; outside agencies as supporting partners |
| **Meeting Needs/Compassion** | Workers serving community to open gospel doors | Local believers serving their own neighbors; dependency risk increases if outsiders remain primary agents |
| **Critical Contextualization** | Workers and local believers working together | Local believers leading; outsiders offering accountability, not answers |
| **Research/Cultural Insights** | Outside researchers learning the people group | Local insiders as primary knowledge holders; research informs their strategy |
| **Multiplying Efforts** | Workers establishing reproducible patterns | Local churches reproducing those patterns on their own initiative |
| **Training/Equipping** | Outside practitioners delivering training | Local practitioners delivering training; outside trainers exit or become coaches |
| **Marketplace Involvement** | Outside workers using BAM for access | Local believers as primary marketplace witnesses in their own community |

### The Outside Worker's Residual Role (Phases 4–7)

Foundations is clear: the missionary's normal role is *not* to plant a church and then pastor it, but to plant and then raise up local leaders. After Phase 4, the outside worker's legitimate functions are:
- **Coaching and encouragement** of local leaders (not directing)
- **Accountability partnership** — not authority
- **Theological resourcing** when requested, pointed back to Scripture and local discernment
- **Facilitating connection** to the broader body of Christ
- **Preparing for Exit to Partnership** — the explicit goal is to work themselves out of a role

Any Accelerator application in Phases 4–7 that re-centers the outside worker as the primary agent is working against Foundations' framework. This is the most common failure mode in late-phase engagement: outside workers who continue to lead rather than release.

## Integration Synthesis

The 12 Accelerators and the 6-component Missionary Task are **complementary, not competing.** The Missionary Task describes *what missionaries do* in sequence. The Accelerators describe *what can catalyze movement* at any given moment. Together:

- The Missionary Task provides the **sequence and structure**
- The Phases provide the **current position**
- The Accelerators provide the **diagnostic and catalytic questions**
- Foundations provides the **non-negotiable constraints**
- The **Phase 4 threshold** marks the transfer of primary agency to local believers — every Accelerator must be applied through that lens from Phase 4 onward

A healthy use of this integrated framework:
1. Establish where the people group is (Phase)
2. Determine who the primary agents are (outside workers pre-Phase 4; local believers Phase 4+)
3. Understand what the Missionary Task requires at this phase
4. Use Accelerator diagnostics to identify gaps and opportunities
5. Apply Foundations guardrails — including the indigeneity standard — before finalizing any strategy
6. Pray throughout — not as step one, but as the posture of the whole process
